The Why

Brain surgery is hard.
Learning it shouldn't be.

Neurosurgery residency is seven years of relentless case exposure — trauma bays at 3am, tumour boards on Friday afternoons, spine emergencies on Christmas morning. The knowledge accumulates in layers, case by case, clue by clue.

That's exactly what Dandle tries to replicate. Not multiple choice questions. Not lecture slides. A patient walks in. You read the history. You make a call. Each wrong answer reveals the next layer — the exam, the imaging, the labs, the intraoperative finding — just like a real case unfolding in real time.

"The good surgeon knows how. The great surgeon knows when. The wise surgeon knows why."

— paraphrased from neurosurgical lore

Every case in Dandle is anchored to a real society guideline — AANS, CNS, BTF, NASS, AHA/ASA, WHO CNS5 — so the teaching point you read after the case isn't just trivia. It's the same framework your attending uses when deciding whether to operate.

And yes, each case links to Anki tags so you can immediately pull the relevant flashcards. Because learning should compound, not fragment.

The Namesake
🧠
Walter Edward Dandy
APRIL 6, 1886 — APRIL 19, 1946

Born in Sedalia, Missouri, Walter Dandy trained under Harvey Cushing at Johns Hopkins before eventually surpassing his mentor's operative speed and technical brilliance — a fact that did not endear him to Cushing. Their rivalry became one of medicine's great feuds, fuelled by ego, genius, and genuine disagreement about how to operate on the human brain.

Dandy invented pneumoencephalography and ventriculography — the first ways to image the brain before MRI existed. He performed the first successful surgery for a cerebral aneurysm. He described the Dandy–Walker malformation. He pioneered surgery for trigeminal neuralgia, acoustic neuromas, and pineal tumours. He did all of this before CT scans, before the operating microscope, before modern anaesthesia.

He was also, by most accounts, an extremely difficult person to work with. Which is, perhaps, very on-brand for a neurosurgeon.
